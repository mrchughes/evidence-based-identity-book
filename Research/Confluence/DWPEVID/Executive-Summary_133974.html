DCS Evidence-Based Identity Platform : Executive Summary

Created by Christian Hughes, last modified on Sept 20, 2025

The Problem

The imaginary Department of Citizen Services should implement a fundamental transformation from its current document-centric evidence management to a unified evidence-based inference engine that would maintain familiar interfaces for citizens and caseworkers while enabling sophisticated uncertainty management and cross-benefit coordination beneath the surface.

DCS currently operates shared document storage capabilities that enable basic file sharing across BasicSupport, FamilyAssist, and AgeCare benefit lines. However, evidence ingestion occurs through separate product-specific routes that apply different business metadata and ontological interpretations before documents reach shared storage. Each product maintains local metadata stores with incompatible schemas, while a central Service for Citizen Information (SCI) publication service shares derived attributes rather than raw evidence, creating semantic confusion when identical attribute names carry different meanings across products.

This fragmented architecture generates systematic problems including 15% error rates in cross-product cases, 2% fraud detection effectiveness for multi-product schemes, and 23% duplicate evidence requests for citizens applying across benefit lines. External partner integration compounds these challenges through diverse ontological frameworks and identification mechanisms that prevent effective evidence correlation across organizational boundaries.

Previous Attempts to Solve

Our evidence-based identity platform directly addresses the fundamental flaws that have doomed centralized ontology standardization efforts across government. While traditional approaches have attempted—and consistently failed—to force diverse systems into rigid semantic standards, our decentralized semantic approach embraces diversity while enabling interoperability through translation rather than standardization.

The stark reality revealed in the UK government's 2025 State of Digital Government Review is undeniable: despite decades of effort, the Data Standards Authority has published only 16 data standards, missing even basic elements like "standardized ways of recording people's names." This failure isn't from lack of trying—it's from attempting the fundamentally impossible task of centrally standardizing ontologies across hundreds of government services with distinct legal mandates and operational requirements. The result: £45-87 billion in annual unrealized benefits representing one of the largest institutional failures in modern government history.

While traditional approaches have created dangerous coupling effects that reduce system adaptability and create brittle dependencies, our platform implements semantic translation that preserves departmental autonomy and policy flexibility. Traditional centralized event architectures introduce catastrophic single points of failure, with central message brokers creating performance bottlenecks that degrade as transaction volumes scale. In contrast, our decentralized event architecture enables peer-to-peer event propagation with cryptographic integrity, eliminating these bottlenecks while allowing each department to maintain sovereignty over its data flows.

Our approach aligns with proven successful implementations: Estonia's X-Road system connecting 450+ organizations without centralized standardization, Buenos Aires' blockchain identity system serving 3.6 million residents through decentralized credentials, and the EU Digital Identity Wallet initiative mandated by eIDAS 2.0. These systems demonstrate that semantic interoperability through decentralized standards achieves what forced centralization cannot: functioning, sustainable government data sharing that preserves both departmental autonomy and citizen privacy.

By separating evidence accumulation from policy interpretation and implementing confidence-based decision authority, our platform eliminates the governance complexity, stakeholder resistance, and exponential compliance costs that have doomed centralization efforts. We create a system that works with reality rather than fighting against it—delivering the coordinated citizen experience that has eluded previous transformation efforts while respecting the inherent diversity that makes government services responsive to distinct citizen needs.

The Transformation Opportunity

The proposed evidence-based identity platform would treat all information as probabilistic assertions of "X said Y about Z" with comprehensive metadata about verification context, confidence levels, and semantic relationships. This approach would acknowledge that government decisions must be made with incomplete information while providing transparent frameworks for managing uncertainty that current binary verification approaches cannot accommodate.

The platform would transform DCS operations through five critical capabilities that address current limitations while building foundations for future service innovation. Evidence accumulation would enable citizens to provide information once with automatic reuse across relevant services, eliminating the 23% re-submission rate that characterizes current operations. Probabilistic identity resolution would correlate evidence across different digital identities and external partner identification systems while preserving citizen privacy choices about service separation.

Semantic translation capabilities would enable evidence verified for one benefit to be appropriately interpreted for other benefits through formal ontology mappings, preserving legitimate policy differences while eliminating duplicate verification effort. Confidence-based processing would enable graduated automation based on evidence quality rather than conservative binary decisions that delay legitimate claims. Cross-system intelligence would identify fraud patterns spanning multiple benefits while enabling proactive service delivery based on evidence patterns that indicate changing citizen needs.

Proposed Technical Architecture

The platform would implement microservices architecture with comprehensive event-driven communication, enabling independent scaling and deployment while maintaining strict data consistency and audit requirements. Apache Jena Fuseki triple stores would provide semantic reasoning capabilities over RDF graphs containing all evidence assertions, identity clusters, and confidence metadata, while operational databases would cache frequently accessed information for performance optimization.

Eight core microservices would handle evidence ingestion, identity resolution, semantic translation, award calculation, investigation queue management, notification delivery, audit compliance, and external integration. Each service would implement sophisticated capabilities while maintaining clear interfaces and comprehensive audit trails that support both operational efficiency and democratic accountability requirements.

The technical foundation would use proven enterprise technologies including Kubernetes for orchestration, Apache Kafka for event streaming, and semantic web standards for interoperability, minimizing implementation risk while enabling advanced capabilities that distinguish the platform from conventional government systems.

Strategic Benefits and Impact

Implementation would deliver measurable improvements across citizen experience, operational efficiency, and organizational capability while positioning DCS as a leader in evidence-based government service delivery. Citizens would experience 80% reductions in duplicate evidence requests, 50% faster processing times for applications involving previously verified information, and proactive service recommendations based on evidence patterns indicating eligibility for additional support.

Operational benefits would include 70% automation of routine retrospective change processing, 40% improvement in fraud detection through cross-benefit correlation, and 25% productivity increases for caseworkers who would focus on complex cases requiring human judgment rather than routine verification tasks. The platform would enable rapid policy implementation without system changes through semantic rule engines that interpret evidence according to updated requirements.

The transformation would create organizational capabilities extending beyond immediate operational improvements. Evidence-based policy development using comprehensive data about intervention effectiveness, cross-government evidence sharing when other departments implement compatible approaches, and adaptive fraud detection that evolves with changing threat patterns would establish DCS as a model for intelligent government service delivery.

Implementation Strategy and Risk Management

The transformation would follow a measured implementation approach that proves value incrementally while managing risks through parallel operation and comprehensive rollback capabilities. Phase 1 would establish core infrastructure and demonstrate evidence reuse within BasicSupport, Phase 2 would extend capabilities to cross-product evidence sharing, Phase 3 would implement advanced analytics and external partner integration, and Phase 4 would enable cross-government evidence sharing pilot programs.

Each implementation phase would deliver concrete benefits while building toward the complete vision, enabling course correction based on operational experience and stakeholder feedback. The approach would minimize political and technical risks while demonstrating transformational benefits that justify continued investment in platform development.

Success would depend on alignment between technological capabilities and organizational change management, with comprehensive training programs, stakeholder engagement, and citizen communication ensuring that platform benefits reach their intended recipients. The evidence-based approach would provide concrete measurement of transformation effectiveness, enabling continuous improvement and evidence-based refinement of implementation strategies.
